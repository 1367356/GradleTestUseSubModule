主题：  Networking:  from conventional to unconventional （网络：传统互连到非传统互连）
 
主讲人：Prof. Edmond A. Jonckheere, University of Southern California
 
时间：　2014年3月20日（周四）下午2:00-4:30
 
地点：　科B302

讲座简介：
     Network science emerged during those days when the Internet was growing at phenomenal speed. The need to develop a science of very large networks became pressing. In a first wave of research, this led to such concepts as Scale-Fee, Small-World, and Erdös-Rényi graphs. In a second wave of research, the need to explain congestion led to the concept of negatively curved networks in the sense of Gromov, a Russian-French mathematician. The third wave came with wireless networks, where a new protocol, the Heat Diffusion (HD) protocol, emphasized a different curvature concept: the Ollivier-Ricci curvature. More recently, the Ollivier-Ricci curvature emerged in the outstanding minor-embeddability of a problem graph in quantum computer architecture. The final twist has recently come out of quantum networks, where the odd features of quantum mechanics led to the “anti-congestion” phenomenon.The lecture will be divided into 4 main parts: 1. Conventional networks: Least cost path routing, Gromov negatively curved networks, congestion, load balancing by curvature control. 2. Toward unconventional network problems: Wireless networks, Back-Pressure (BP) and Heat-Diffusion (HD) protocols, undirected versus directed graphs, Riemannian versus Finsler manifolds, Heat Diffusion with directionality constraints, nonlinear Laplacian, Ollivier-Ricci curvature versus capacity region. 3. Truly unconventional networks: Quantum chains as quantum wires, towards dynamically controlled routing in quantum rings; quantum routers, “anti-congestion” phenomenon in quantum chains. 4. Into the future: Maximum Ollivier-Ricci curvature of qubit architecture for adiabatic quantum computations.
 
个人简介:
    Edmond A. Jonckheere, Full Professor of the University of Southern California received the Electrical Engineer degree from the Universite de Louvain, Louvain-la-Neuve, Belgium, in 1973; the Doctor in Engineering degree in Aerospace Engineering from the Universite Paul Sabatier, Toulouse, France, in 1975; and the Ph.D. degree in Electrical Engineering from the University of Southern California, Los Angeles, in 1978. From 1975 to 1978, he was a Fulbright/Hays Fellow and a Teaching and Research Assistant in the Department of Electrical Engineering--Systems and subsequently a Research Associate in the same department. From 1978 to 1979 he was with the Royal Military Academy, Brussels, Belgium. From 1979 to 1980, he was with the Philips Research Laboratory, Brussels, Belgium. In 1980, he returned to the University of Southern California, where he is currently a Full Professor of Electrical Engineering and Mathematics, an Associate Member of the Center for Applied Mathematical Sciences (CAMS), and a member of the newly established Center for Computer Systems Security (CCSS) of the Information Sciences Institute (ISI) of the University of Southern California. He is currently an Associate Editor of the IEEE Transactions on Automatic Control and was elected Fellow of the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers in 1991, for Contributions to the spectral theory of linear-quadratic and H-infinity control. In 1999, he was listed in the Marquis Who's Who in Science and Engineering. His most recent research interests are in Cyber security, network traffic modeling for Active Queue Management (AQM) and Random Early Drop (RED), hyperbolic geometry of networks, and worm propagation.
